Carbohydrate-Active Enzymes (CAZymes) in Lignocellulosic Biomass Conversion for Bioethanol Production: Recent Advances and Biotechnological Applications
Bharati Baruah*
Department of Bioengineering and Technology, Gauhati University.
*Corresponding author: Bharati Baruah; E-mail address: bharati19@gauhati.ac.in;
Tel: +91,9864503407
Received: April 13, 2025; Revised: June 07, 2025; Accepted: 25 June 2025
  Volume-17(2025) Issue-02(DOI: 10.13140/RG.2.2.24655.39840) application/pdf 661.45 KB View
Full Length Paper
Abstract:

The increasing global energy demand, coupled with the need for sustainable alternatives, has intensified the focus on renewable energy sources. Biofuels produced from carbon-based biomass are gaining prominence, particularly for the ample availability of raw materials. Amongst the different types of biofuels, the second-generation (2G) biofuels, which are derived from non-food lignocellulose biomass (LCB) show significant promise due to their sustainability and less negative environmental impact. However, the recalcitrant nature of LCB acts as a major hindrance in using it as a source of biofuel. The CAZymes are a class of enzymes that play a crucial role in breaking the lignin and complex carbohydrates by modifying the glycosidic bond, removing esters and hydrolysis. Out of the five major classes of CAZymes - the glycoside hydrolase, carbohydrate esterase and auxiliary activities proteins are primarily involved in the deconstruction of LCB into simple unit sugars which are further fermented to bioethanol. Though abundantly found in nature, these enzymes are being engineered with modern biotechnological tools to efficiently convert LCB into biofuels at minimum costs. This review focuses on the role of CAZymes in bioethanol production and also discusses the strategies used to modify this class of carbohydrate modifying enzymes using modern computational, multi-omics and enzyme engineering tools and to increase the production of renewable and sustainable sources of energy from LCB.

Keywords: CAZymes; Lignocellulose biomass; Bioethanol; Enzyme engineering; Enzyme cocktails.

Isolation and characterization of protease-producing bacteria from the hulls of Vigna radiata
Manswama Boro, Vidisha Gogoi, and Anil Kumar Verma*
Department of Microbiology, Sikkim University, Gangtok-737102, Sikkim, India.
*Corresponding author: Dr. Anil Kumar Verma; E-mail: akverma@cus.ac.in;

Received: April 14, 2025; Revised: June 24, 2025; Accepted: June 28, 2025

Full Length Paper
Abstract:

The widespread and extensive use of chemicals in industries has led to serious environmental and health concerns, prompting scientists to search for natural alternatives, such as microbial enzymes. This study explores protease-producing bacteria from discarded mung bean hulls (an underutilized agricultural waste product). After screening, two strains (D15 and D18) exhibiting the highest protease enzyme activity (8.01 U/mL and 5.70 U/mL, respectively) were selected for further analysis from six protease enzyme-producing isolates obtained from dried, moist, and fresh hull samples. Optimal protease production occurred at 25–30°C and pH 7, with mung bean hulls proving an effective low-cost waste substrate (yielding up to 17.82 U/mL). Genetic identification revealed that the isolate D15 was Serratia marcescens, and the isolate D18 was Chrysobacterium sp. Crude protease enzyme from D15 was found to be practically applicable in removing 5% sericin during the silk degumming process, which proved to be an environmentally friendly approach compared to traditional, energy-consuming procedures. The isolates also showed multifunctional properties with their ability to produce other industrially important enzymes (xylanase, cellulase). This work not only provides an insight into the valorization of agricultural waste but also provides a scalable, eco-friendly approach for industrially important enzyme production with significant applications in textiles and waste management.

Keywords: Protease enzymes; Mung bean hulls; Serratia marcescens; Silk degumming; Waste valorization

Abstract:

As standard petroleum-based plastics create a severe environmental impact have led to a growing demand for sustainable and ecofriendly materials in various industries. Bioplastics are rapidly developing class of polymers to meet both commercial and industrial needs are the promising alternative to the conventional plastics. In the present study, Bioplastics were developed by utilizing non-value food byproducts as sources which are of low-cost and easily available, that is Spent coffee ground, Cluster bean, Areca husk fibers and Essential oil. Polysaccharide rich fractions were retrieved from the spent coffee ground whilst Galactomannan isolated from the Cluster bean endosperm which gives high viscosity and provides flexibility to the Bioplastics. To increase the tensile strength, the Areca husk fibers were added as the source of cellulose and essential oil extracted from the Pomelo fruit peel enhances the shelf life. To obtain the feasible bioplastic, the extracted components were combined in a certain ratio with glycerol and gelatin. Several tests such as, biodegradability, antimicrobial, tensile strength, combustion and morphological studies by Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) and Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) were performed. The bioplastic developed in the present study showed better tensile strength and reduced carbon emission hence it may have positive effect on environment. FTIR and SEM analysis showed characteristic features of bioplastics. Hence, the study addresses that management of beverage and agricultural waste in development of sustainable galactomannan-based bioplastics.

Keywords: Biodegradable; Bioplastic; Cellulose; Ecofriendly; Essential oil; Galactomannan.

Abstract:

Camel milk, often referred to as "gold of the desert," possesses significant anti-tubercular, anticancer and hypoglycemic properties. In continuation to our previous studies, camel milk was collected and then processed using modified method of Kobata and Ginsburg to isolate its oligosaccharide contents. Acetylation and chromatographic purification resulted in isolation of a novel pentasaccharide, named Eluose (C32H55O26N). Structure elucidation of Eluose was done using advanced 2D NMR spectroscopic experiments like HSQC, TOCSY, COSY and HMBC. The HSQC spectrum at 800 MHz of acetylated Eluose showed six cross-peaks, indicating the presence of six anomeric protons and carbons, suggesting it to be a pentasaccharide in its reducing form. Further, TOCSY analysis helped in distinguishing ¹H chemical shifts of each monosaccharide proton, while COSY spectra sequenced their position in the ring. The configuration and conformation of glycosidic linkages were established through 1H anomeric proton splitting patterns, HMBC and HSQC data confirmed glycosidic linkages while anomeric proton splitting patterns also helped in establishing their configuration and conformation. Abstract:

Cervical cancer is one of the most common malignancies affecting women worldwide, particularly in developing countries, where it poses a major public health challenge. Persistent infection with high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) types has been identified as a primary causative agent. Although conventional cervical cytology has contributed significantly to early detection, its diagnostic sensitivity remains inconsistent. In recent years, the field of glycobiology has provided new insights into the molecular underpinnings of cervical cancer. Changes in glycosylation patterns—particularly alterations in N-linked and terminal glycans— have been linked to tumor progression and metastasis. These glycosylation changes influence several biological processes, including cell adhesion, immune recognition, and intracellular signaling, all of which are critical to cancer development. Modern analytical approaches now enable precise monitoring of glycosylation in serum and tissue, making glycan profiling a valuable tool for biomarker discovery. Advances in glycoproteomic technologies have enhanced the reproducibility and cost-effectiveness of such analyses, opening new avenues for improved diagnostics and therapeutic interventions in cervical cancer.

Unveiling the Affinity of Gluco-Oligosaccharides for Bacterial L-Lactate Dehydrogenase Interactions Essential for Probiotic Modulation

3Department of Pharmaceutical Sciences, Dibrugarh University, Dibrugarh-786004, Assam, India.

Full Length Paper
Abstract:

Gluco-oligosaccharides (GlcOS) are typical of non-digestible dietary fibres. They are considered as potential prebiotic compounds due to their ability to modulate gut microbiota, which provides health benefits to the host. This study aimed to evaluate the safety, bioavailability, and selected gut microbial (Bifidobacterium longum, Lactococcus lactis, and Lactobacillus casei) interaction of GlcOS. 14 GlcOS were chosen for this study. Among these 14 GlcOS, trehalose, neotrehalose, nigerose, and laminaribiose were found to be potential prebiotic agents following computational analyses, such as ADMET, which showed they possess high water solubility, low gastrointestinal absorption, and are non-permeable to the blood-brain barrier. Toxicological determination predicted these GlcOS to be inactive for hepatotoxicity, immunotoxicity, mutagenicity, carcinogenicity, and cytotoxicity, suggesting good safety profiles. Among these, trehalose, neotrehalose, and nigerose are categorized under toxicity Class 6, indicating minimal acute toxicity and excellent safety profiles. Molecular docking studies further demonstrated that several GlcOS, especially neotrehalose, have strong binding affinities (5.92 kcal/mol) with L-lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) of Bifidobacterium longum, followed by Nigerose (binding affinities -4.99 kcal/mol) with Lactococcus lactis LDH protein. Trehalose showed binding affinities (-3.49 kcal/mol) with LDH of Bifidobacterium longum, and Isomaltose had binding affinities (-3.39 kcal/mol) with LDH of Lactobacillus casei. These outcomes are equivalent to standard 1-Kestose and Inulin. The interactions of GlcOS with the protein LDH of gut microbiota suggest that GlcOS may have a modulatory function in enhancing gut health and microbial metabolic activity. The potential of GlcOS is supported by the combined insights from molecular docking, toxicity prediction, and ADMET analysis, which support safe and effective prebiotics. Their selective microbial interactions and good safety profiles suggest potential uses in producing gut microbiome-targeted therapeutics and dietary supplements.

Keywords: Prebiotics; Gluco-oligosaccharides; L-lactate dehydrogenase; Molecular Docking; Molecular Dynamics
